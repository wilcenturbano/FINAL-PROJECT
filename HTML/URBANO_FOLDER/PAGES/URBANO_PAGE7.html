SELF CONCEPTUALIZATION

Malleable Self-Concept



The ability of ourselves to invoke specific self-schemas while ignoring others, our self-concepts are malleable. At any given time, our self-concept is influenced by the social situations in which we find ourselves belongs, and the feedback, we receive from our surroundings is very important to how we perceive ourselves. In some cases, this malleability means that certain aspects of the self will be more visible than others. A 14-year-old, for example, may become aware of her youth when she is with a group of elderly people. If the same 14-year-old was in a group of other teenagers, she would be much less likely to consider her age because it is somehow normal for them.

Baumeister provides the following self-concept definition: "The individual's belief about himself or herself, including the person's attributes and who and what the self is".

The Existential Self

This is the most basic part of the self-scheme or self-concept; the sense of being separate and distinct from others and the awareness of the constancy of the self' (Bee, 1992). The child discovers that they are distinct from others and that their life transcends time and space. According to Lewis, knowledge of the existential self starts as early as two to three months of age and is influenced in part by the child's relationship with the environment. For example, a child may smile and receive a reciprocal smile, or a child may touch a smartphone and see it move.

The Categorical Self

After realizing that he or she is a separate experiencing entity, the child then realized that he or she is also an object in this world. The child is becoming aware of himself or herself as an entity who has an individuality that can be observed they need to learn how to embrace themselves and learn to accept that each individual has unique traits that make them stand out to others.
